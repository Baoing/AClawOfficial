New stores often sprint straight to apps, themes, and “AI features” because those feel like progress. The stores that stay healthy six months later almost always do the opposite: they stabilize **how money, inventory, and markets behave** inside Shopify before they decorate the storefront or wire clever automation.

This guide is a week-by-week shape of that path. It mirrors how we onboard teams in real projects—catalog truth first, internal automation second, customer-facing AI last.

### Week one: trust the platform defaults

Your first week should feel almost administrative because it is. Payments, shipping zones, taxes, and markets need to reflect how you actually sell, not how you hope to sell someday. When these foundations are wrong, every later decision—discounts, scripts, Flow, even AI copy—inherits the error.

We encourage teams to write down three numbers they care about weekly: gross sales, refund rate, and fulfillment aging. If Shopify’s native reports cannot produce those cleanly yet, fix data entry and configuration before you buy another dashboard.

### Week two: catalog truth in Shopify objects

Merchandising lives in products, variants, collections, and metafields. If “lead time,” “compliance copy,” or “B2B-only SKUs” only exist in spreadsheets, your storefront and automation will eventually disagree with reality. Moving attributes into typed fields is tedious work, but it is the work that makes Flow, search, and recommendations trustworthy.

This is also the week to decide how you name tags and how you segment customers. Small inconsistencies here become expensive arguments between marketing and operations later.

### Week three: introduce Flow for internal hygiene

Once orders look sane, add Flow for **internal** reliability: notify Slack on high-value orders, tag fraudy patterns, route wholesale quotes for approval, or pause fulfillment when a supplier flags a lot. These flows rarely touch shoppers directly, which makes them a safe training ground.

Keep each flow readable and named like a runbook chapter. If only one person understands a flow, it is not finished.

### AI after the data model is honest

Only after catalog and operations behave should you add AI for drafting PDPs, summarizing internal threads, or flagging catalog drift. Models amplify whatever structure you give them. A messy catalog becomes confidently wrong copy; a clean metafield model becomes faster edits with fewer reverts.

Keep approvals on for anything customer-facing, and log prompt versions when you fine-tune internal tools. You will thank yourself during the first compliance review.

### Definition of done for “phase one”

Phase one is not “the theme looks good on a staging URL.” Done means refunds behave, inventory locations behave under split shipments, and at least one Flow is monitored weekly. That foundation is what makes later app and AI work feel cheap instead of fragile.
